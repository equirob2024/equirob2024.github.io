Equivariant Robotics: The Role of Symmetry Across Perception, Estimation, and Control

IROS 2024 workshop, Abu Dhabi
October 14 2024, 08:30-12:30

Towards the end of robotic systems capable of ubiquitous, persistent deployment in the wild, a growing trend has emerged across the many subdisciplines of our field. Namely, researchers in diverse areas have exploited the inherent symmetries present in robotic systems and their environment to achieve drastic improvements in performance, efficiency, and robustness. Equivariant architectures for data-driven perception have demonstrated impressive generalization and sample efficiency, all the while reducing model complexity and guaranteeing by design that extraneous transformations will not degrade their predictions. Meanwhile, a symmetry-aware approach to state estimation has yielded improved convergence properties, providing both formal certificates and astonishing empirical accuracy. Moreover, as the complexity of both individual agents and multiagent teams has grown, the exploitation of symmetry in control has tamed unwieldy high-dimensional models, sated the appetite for data-hungry methods like reinforcement learning, and aided in the decentralized coordination of large robot swarms. This workshop brings together prominent experts and early-career researchers, working across a wide range of problems, applications, and methodologies in robotics, to share their perspectives on the multifaceted role of equivariance in autonomous systems. Ultimately, this cross-pollination of traditionally distinct research communities will identify new avenues of investigation to leverage these cross-cutting concepts, in pursuit of intelligent robotic systems prepared to tackle society's greatest challenges and play an active role in our daily lives.

Goals and Overview The goal of this workshop is to bring together a technically diverse cohort of researchers working on a wide range of urgent problems in robotics to examine the role of symmetry in the development of efficient, robust, and high-performance solutions to many challenges faced in robotics. As researchers share their long-term progress, latest results, and future goals at the workshop, we expect others to learn new techniques from other disciplines that can be applied to their own domain of interest, thanks to the essential and recurring nature of symmetry in robotics. We also hope that researchers looking for new research problems will be inspired to explore fresh avenues in other subdisciplines in which they can exploit their existing, transferrable geometric expertise, leading to greater interplay between different literatures and forging a broader community. Crucially, we hope that this workshop will also introduce new members of the broader robotics community to symmetry-informed methods and their exciting opportunities with regards to fundamental challenges in robotics, leading to a broader adoption of state of the art techniques and an infusion of new talent into this community. To ensure this ambitious goal is feasible, we will begin the workshop with an equivariance tutorial, a “crash course” meant to make the workshop accessible to a very general audience. Lastly, this workshop will emphasize the close interconnection between symmetry-aware methods and IROS 2024's theme of “Robotics for Sustainable Development”. A major societal concern with extremely large models in machine learning and artificial intelligence is the environmental impact of the astronomical energy cost of training these models. The improved sample efficiency and reduced model complexity of symmetry-aware architectures represents an enticing opportunity to mitigate these externalities by matching or exceeding the performance of existing models while reducing their environmental burden. Moreover, improvements in state estimation and motion control via symmetry exploitation lead to greater reliability, robustness, and durability of autonomous systems, reducing losses in real-world deployments. This minimizes materials use and production impacts of sustained deployment of large robot teams in the wild, and also nets capable autonomous systems, better prepared to take part in a thriving green economy and overcome environmental stewardship challenges.
